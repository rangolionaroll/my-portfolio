Aerobanquets RMX

A virtual reality dining experience presented by Superblue Miami and Meta Open Arts as during Miami Art Week in November 2022.

Role: Developer


Aerobanquets RMX is a virtual-reality art and culinary experience created by artist Mattia Casalegno in collaboration with Michelin-starred chef Chintan Pandya of Semma, Dhamaka, and Adda in New York. Aerobanquets RMX is an immersive, multisensorial, multicourse meal where guests explore, eat, and engage with a virtual world. Read more about the experience here.

This was one of the projects I worked on as a contract worker at Pariah Interactive and was a 6-week long project. This project was built in Unity using Leap Motion for hand tracking and Meta Quest 2 as the virtual reality headset. Before this project most of my experience had been with developing a project from scratch, based on a storyboard or design. This was the first project in my professional career where I had to pick up from where someone else left off and add to their work, which involved a lot of structuring the flow and architecture of the project, and diving into Unity Timelines, to try to create a seamless experience.


Eating Detection

    Along with restructuring the flow and architecture of the project I got to work on a few cool interactions using the Leap Motion and Quest Controllers. One of which was eating detection. Previously, servers would place the food on each user’s plate and then after the user had eaten, they would press a button to progress the experience.

    To automate this, and reduce the server’s workload, I added a detection feature where the proximity of the plate (Quest controller) and the mouth (Quest headset) would be checked along with the inclination of both, as most users would pick up the plate, bring it to their mouth and then tilt it back to drop the food in. To do this, I did various trials with foods such as trail mix, chocolate and snacks and recorded the inclination and distance at the moment the food fell into my mouth. Based on these values I was able to isolate the axis on which the controller and headset tilted as well as determine a range for the values of rotation and distance. I did learn that I eat at a higher angle than many of my coworkers so I had to iterate on the range after doing QA tests with them but eventually got to a reliable range.

Hand Interactions

Using the leap motion, I mainly focused on two different types of interactions. One of which was grabbing and throwing jelly-like objects and the other being objects floating away from the user as they approached them. Using Leap Motion’s built in interaction system, most of my work was changing the physics on the objects themselves. To grab and throw jelly-like objects I used Unity’s soft body physics system.

The pushing objects away on hover was more interesting because I tried to get the direction of the force being applied based on the direction of the hand and settled on focusing on the index finger as I found the usual approach people took with interacting with a floating object would be to poke it first and and also used Keijiro’s Procedural Motion package to add some movement before the person interacted with the objects.
