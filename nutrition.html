Kindergarten Nutrition Games

This project is a series of 2D games designed for the Rutgers Culture of Health School Program.
Currently 3 of 7 expected games have been developed.

Roles: Project Lead, Developer, Designer

Introduction

This project consists of 7 games that each cover a Nutrition topic and have been designed for a kindergarten age group. Currently, three of these games have been developed and shipped. As project lead for these three games, I have seen them through, from scoping to delivery. While each of these games had individual lesson objectives, overall they had the goal of understanding how well a student was able to grasp the content and what ideas they struggled with. Read about the individual games below.

Skelly's Search for Dairy

For this game another team member came up with the concept. This included the two options for each question, the meter that counts progress, the skeleton mascot and the story of the skelly needing to power up to go play.

I worked on taking the concept and completing it. I mapped out all the game flows to make sure no pieces were missing as shown on the left and then used that storyboard to collaborate with the artist and sound designer to organize the art and sounds.

For example, the original storyboard showed the question and then showed a large coin on the screen to indicate they had gotten the question right. I completed this flow by making the coin appear over the correct answer and fly to the skeleton meter. It then became a snack that filled up the meter.


    Along with completing the game flows, an important part of this project was an assessment of the students playing the game. I decided to accomplish this in two ways.

  • The first was through a certificate activity. With a certificate at the end of the game, the teachers could assess whether a student had completed the game. It also served as a fun takeaway for the students, which they could save and print. Rather than a plain certificate, I decided that it would be cool to add an activity to customize it. For this game, that was coloring in Skelly and also decorating with different dairy products. The storyboard and the UI that I created are shown below.

  • The second was through custom events during the game. I designed and developed the framework of the game to be able to collect information for things such as how long a child took per question, to get a better idea of how the game is being played and what questions children struggled with most.


Let's Learn About Plants

For this game I came up with the original concept. One of the storyboard images is shown. The original concept had two portions, a storybook and a quiz. With the storybook, the children would move along the story by interacting with environmental elements such as the sun. We decided to shorten the game and take away the first activity.

For the quiz section, the idea was that a food element would be introduced, and that the child would have to identify which element it is, on a generic plant. They would do this by clicking that part on the plant. After working with the designer, we found that for parts that are scattered on the plant, this might be a bit difficult, so we added tags that the player could interact with. A sample of our brainstorming is shown.

To keep the introduction interesting as the parts of a plant are being introduced, we kept the buildup element but did that with the sound design. With each plant part, a new instrument is introduced to the soundtrack and then later in the quiz section those same sounds are used to reinforce each plant part. An example of this is shown in the video.


    Along with the inital concept, similarly to the first game, an important part of this game was the assessment. This was also accomplished in two ways.

  • The first was through the certificate activity. Like the previous game, the certificate served both to assess completion and as a fun takeaway for the students. For this game, the actvity was to create their own plant. They had the option to pick a design for each part of the plant. The storyboard and the completed product are shown below.

  • The second was also through custom events during the game. I designed and developed the framework of the game to be able to collect information for things such as what incorrect answers a student was clicking, to get a better idea of what foods children found most confusing.


Gill's Diner: MyPlate Food Groups

For this game I came up with the original concept. To change things up with how the game would be played, I wanted to use a drag and drop concept rather than clicking the correct option. For this game, I decided to have the food be dragged onto the plate into the correct food group. With one of the introductory prompts being about smiling, I also wanted the mascot to be a shark for its many teeth. Since the concept was about building plates using the myPlate food groups, I also wanted him to be a chef.

With this information our lead designer, came up with the concept of the shark working in a diner to build the plates. He worked with me to rework the UI and the story so that it hit all the points I scoped and would also be fast to develop. The first concept sketch and the final product are shown.


    Along with the inital concept, similarly to the other games, an important part of this game was the assessment. This was also accomplished in two ways.

  • The first was through the certificate activity. Like the previous games, the certificate served both to assess completion and as a fun takeaway for the students. For this game, the actvity was to create a menu for the diner. For this menu, they would be picking a daily special for each food category. The storyboard and the completed product are shown below.

  • The second was also through custom events during the game. I designed and developed the framework of the game to be able to collect information for things such as what incorrect answers a student was selecting, to get a better idea of what foods children found most confusing. Unlike the other games, answers get eliminated as the game is played, so to still collect information, the order of the foods was randomized and collected.
